Overview

CCO Capstone is a browser-based training app for Air Force Contingency Contracting Officers. It runs three coordinated dashboards — Trainer (who fires injects and responds as the white cell), Student (who plays the CCO/ACO/leader and handles the inbox), and Inspector (the observer who grades performance).

This Builder is the authoring tool. You use it to design a scenario — a sequence of injected events, organized into phases, with role players and expected actions — that the trainer then runs from the Trainer dashboard. Think of it as a Master Scenario Events List (MSEL) editor with a visual timeline on top. If you've ever built an MSEL for an IG exercise, you already know how to use it.

Scenarios live in your browser's local storage while you're working on them, and you export them to a .json file when you want to share, back up, or hand off to a successor.

Build your first scenario — the five-step loop

  1. Set the scenario header. At the top of the Builder, give your scenario a title, a one-line mission intent, the number of days, and the day start/end hours. A typical CCO rotation is 3–5 days of exercise clock, running from 0800 to 1800.
  2. Add phases. Click "Add phase" and create the major scene-shifts. Example: Phase I — First day on the job (D1 0800), Phase II — Marines arrive / bare-base standup (D2 0900), Phase III — Closeouts and redeployment (D4 0800). Multiple phases per day are fine. Phases redirect the focus of the exercise, not just the clock.
  3. Add injects. Click "+ New inject" in the palette, or click any time slot on the Timeline to create one at that time. Fill in the role player, method, event description, and expected action. Tag the inject to a phase if it only makes sense inside that phase.
  4. Playtest. Click "MSEL Table" to see your scenario as a sortable grid. Read it top-to-bottom as if you were the student. Does each inject have a clear expected action? Is there enough breathing room between them? Do the phases actually redirect focus or are they just clock changes?
  5. Export. Click "Export JSON" to save the scenario to a file. Click "Print MSEL" to produce a formal MSEL document you can hand to an approving authority or file with your exercise package.

What makes a good inject (and what makes a bad one)

A good inject has all five of these:

  1. A specific time. Not "sometime Day 2." A specific day, hour, and minute. If you don't know exactly when, put your best guess and iterate.
  2. A real role player with voice. "Lt Col Ramsey, hot under the collar, wants to know why the generator mod hasn't been signed" is an inject. "Someone asks about the generator" is not.
  3. A clear event. The student should be able to read the inject and understand what just happened without having to ask clarifying questions.
  4. A concrete expected action. One or more specific things a competent CCO would do in response. "Apply judgment" is not an expected action. "Pull the contract file, verify the funding line is O&M and not OCO, and walk the mod to the contracting officer for signature by 1500" is.
  5. A teaching point. Why does this inject exist? What lesson is it delivering? If you can't answer that, the inject is probably noise and should be cut.

Bad injects are usually one of three things: vague (no clear event, no clear action), trivia-testing (tests whether the student memorized a FAR reference instead of whether they can reason under pressure), or unfair (requires information the student has no way to know). When in doubt, cut the inject and write a better one.

Rule of thumb: a 4-day exercise should have 40–60 injects. More than that and the students drown; fewer and they're bored. Adjust based on team size and experience level.

Phases — the scene-shifts that make it feel real

A phase is a major shift in the type of work the students are doing. It's not just a clock mark — it redirects what's on their plate.

Example phase stack for a bare-base deployment exercise:

  • Phase I — First day on the job (D1 0800). Students inherit a messy file pile from the outgoing CCO. Focus: triage, orientation, figuring out who's who, finding the ratifications and expired contracts hiding in the files.
  • Phase II — Marines arrive, bare-base standup (D2 0900). 600 inbound personnel, nothing's ready. Focus: BPAs, emergency purchases, fuel contracts, standing up a K-shop tracker, working with the host nation.
  • Phase III — Steady state / compliance pressure (D3 0800). IG inspection looming, congressional delegation visit, press interest. Focus: documentation quality, ratification cleanup, dealing with scrutiny while still executing the mission.
  • Phase IV — Closeouts and redeployment (D4 0800). Contracts ending, property accountability, transition to the incoming unit. Focus: proper closeout procedures, funds returns, ensuring nothing falls through the cracks.

A phase change should noticeably alter what's in the inbox. If students can't tell from the injects alone that the phase shifted, the phase isn't doing its job.

White Cell support — use it sparingly

The White Cell support needed flag marks injects that require a live human to role-play — taking a phone call, walking into the room as an angry vendor, responding in real time to unexpected follow-up questions. These are the highest-impact injects because they feel real, but they're also the most expensive to run because they pull white-cell personnel away from the rest of the exercise.

Rule of thumb: 10–20% of your injects should be white-cell. More than that and the exercise becomes a logistics problem; fewer and the whole thing feels flat. The Builder's legend shows white-cell injects with a yellow ring on the timeline so you can eyeball the distribution.

When you flag an inject as white-cell, write specific delivery notes in the event description — "deliver calmly, escalate if pushed on timeline" — so whoever plays the role doesn't have to improvise from scratch.

Playtest before you run it on real students

Open your scenario in the MSEL Table view and read it top-to-bottom from the student's perspective. Ask yourself:

  • If I were the student, would I know what just happened when this inject arrives?
  • Do I have enough information (or a clear path to find it) to produce the expected action?
  • Is there any inject that depends on earlier student actions I haven't set up?
  • Are there obvious dead zones with nothing happening?
  • Are there pile-ups where multiple hard injects land at the same time?

Better yet: actually run the scenario against yourself in a browser. Open the Trainer dashboard in one window and the Student dashboard in another, fire injects one by one, and see whether you can answer each one. If you get stuck on an inject you wrote, a real student has no chance.

Export, share, and print

The Builder gives you three ways to get your scenario out:

  • Save writes the current scenario to your browser's local storage. It's automatic after every edit, but clicking the button forces a save and shows a confirmation.
  • Export JSON downloads a .json file that holds everything — meta, phases, injects. Share it via OneDrive, email, or a thumb drive. Anyone with a copy of the Builder can Import JSON to load it back.
  • Print MSEL produces a formal Master Scenario Events List document with a cover sheet, phase summary, full inject table, and detailed per-inject appendix. From the print dialog, choose "Save as PDF" to get a shareable document you can submit to an approving authority.

The JSON format is the canonical format. The PDF is the human-readable version. Keep the JSON under version control or in a shared folder so you never lose it.

Field reference

Event #
A unique identifier for the inject. The Builder auto-generates one if you leave it blank. Using a stable ID means exported scenarios stay referenceable even as titles change.
Event title
A one-line headline. Think "newspaper lede" — the student should understand what happened from the title alone. Example: Vendor walks in with an over-threshold quote.
Role player (From)
The character the inject comes from. Include a name, a rank or role, and a one-line context clue. Example: Lt Col Ramsey, A-2 Plans — hot under the collar.
Method
How the inject is delivered. Email and Text are flat — no white cell needed. Call and Walk-in require a live role player. Document drop means physical or digital paperwork hits the student's workspace.
Day / Hour / Minute
The exercise time at which this inject fires. Day is 1-indexed. Hours are 24-hour. The trainer can still fire it manually out of sequence if needed.
Phase
The scene-shift this inject belongs to. Optional — injects without a phase are assumed to apply across all phases.
Event description
The full narrative of what's happening. Write it from the student's perspective — this is roughly what they'll see in their inbox or hear on the phone. Include the details needed to decide on an action.
Expected action
One line per action the student should take. Be specific. This is the column the observer grades against, so vague expectations mean vague grading.
Documents to push
Any files that should drop into the student's workspace alongside this inject — contracts, quotes, spreadsheets. One filename per line.
White Cell support needed
Flag this if a live role-player must be physically available. See the White Cell section for guidance on when to use it.
Role tag (To)
Who sees this inject. Broadcast goes to everyone. CCO / ACO / Leadership routes to students with that role assignment. Pick Broadcast when in doubt.

Handing off to a successor

If you're reading this because the previous scenario author left, welcome. Here's what you need to know.

Everything lives in this Builder. You don't need a coder, you don't need access to GitHub, you don't need anything you don't already have. Scenarios are just JSON files. The Iron Meridian scenario that ships with the app is a good reference — click "Import stock injects" to load it and see how a mature scenario is structured. Don't be afraid to delete things and rebuild.

Start by playing through the Iron Meridian scenario as a student. Open the Trainer and Student dashboards in two windows, fire injects, respond. You'll get a feel for the cadence in 30 minutes. Then look at the same injects in this Builder's MSEL Table view and you'll see the structure from the author's side.

When you build your own scenario, start from a copy of Iron Meridian rather than a blank page. It's much easier to delete and modify than to write from scratch. Change the scenario title, walk through each inject, and replace or cut the ones that don't fit your mission.

When you're ready to run it, export the JSON, back it up somewhere safe, and distribute the whole app folder to your trainer and students via OneDrive or Teams. Everything they need runs from index.html.

If you get stuck, the previous author's contact info should be in the scenario header. If it isn't, leave your own in there for the next person.
